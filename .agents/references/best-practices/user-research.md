### User Research Best Practices

#### Do

- ✅ Define clear research questions before selecting methods — method follows question, not the reverse
- ✅ Recruit participants who represent actual or target users, including diverse abilities, backgrounds, and experience levels
- ✅ Use 5–8 participants per segment for qualitative usability studies (diminishing returns beyond this)
- ✅ Triangulate findings across multiple methods and data sources to increase confidence
- ✅ Separate observation from interpretation — record what happened before inferring why
- ✅ Obtain informed consent and protect participant privacy; follow ethical research guidelines
- ✅ Share findings promptly and in actionable formats tailored to stakeholder needs
- ✅ Maintain a research repository so insights are discoverable and cumulative
- ✅ Include people with disabilities in research recruiting — at minimum 1 in 4 adults live with a disability
- ✅ Pilot test research instruments (interview guides, surveys, prototypes) before full deployment
- ✅ Credit established frameworks and cite sources when applying published heuristics or methods

#### Avoid

- ❌ Leading questions or priming participants toward desired answers
- ❌ Confirmation bias — designing studies that only validate existing assumptions
- ❌ Relying on a single method or a single round of research to draw conclusions
- ❌ Testing with colleagues or internal staff as a substitute for real user research
- ❌ Presenting anecdotes as evidence — qualify sample sizes and generalizability
- ❌ Delaying research until after implementation decisions are made
- ❌ Conflating usability testing with user acceptance testing (UAT)
- ❌ Ignoring edge cases, assistive technology users, or low-literacy participants in study design
